2:39Module 1 · The trust problem

Slide 5 · 2:39Module one. I want to start with a number, because this number is why this course exists, and almost nobody outside a few research journals knows it yet.

Slide 6 · 2:50In 2023, careful analysts estimated that about one paper in every two thousand eight hundred had a fabricated reference in it. A citation pointing at something that does not exist. This year, a Lancet analysis scanned more than two million papers and ninety-seven million citations, and the number is now about one paper in two hundred seventy-seven. Read that again. A roughly six-fold rise in three years, in the most carefully checked writing humans produce, peer-reviewed science. And the three years in question are exactly the years AI writing assistants went mainstream. That is not a coincidence, and everyone studying it knows it is not.

Slide 7 · 3:32Here is the twist that makes this hard. The AI is not a bad research tool. It is the best research tool ever built. It reads faster than you, summarizes better than most people, and finds real sources in seconds. The problem is that it also produces fake sources in seconds, in the same voice, with the same formatting, with the same confidence. A real find and a pure invention come out of the machine looking identical. You cannot tell them apart by looking, and I will prove that to you in module four.

Slide 8 · 4:10So here is the mental model for this whole course. The AI is a brilliant, tireless research assistant with one dangerous habit: it would rather hand you something that looks right than admit it found nothing. It is not lying, exactly. It completes patterns. When the true answer is missing from its head, a plausible-looking answer fills the gap, fluent and cited and formatted. Your job is not to distrust everything. Your job is to know which claims your work actually rests on, and to check those. That is the entire course in two sentences.

Slide 9 · 4:48The plan, then. Module two is Find: how to get answers with receipts, from tools that actually retrieve before they answer. Module three is Verify: lateral reading, the fastest verification habit ever invented. Module four is Cite: the citation trap, and the three clicks that beat it. Module five is the new deep research tools, spectacular and subtly holey. Module six is judgment: disclosure, honesty, and when checking is worth your time. After this course, the phrase, the AI said so, will never again be your final answer on anything that matters.

5:25Module 2 · Find: receipts, not vibes

Slide 10 · 5:25Module two. Find. There are two kinds of AI answers in the world, and the entire game is knowing which one you are looking at.

Slide 11 · 5:35Kind one: the memory answer. You ask a plain chatbot a question, and it answers from what it absorbed during training. It is reconstructing, from compressed memory, something that sounds like what the answer should be. No live lookup happens. No link exists. It might be right, often it is, but you have no way to check except to believe it. Kind two: the receipt answer. A search-grounded tool takes your question, actually searches the web right now, reads real pages, and writes an answer with citations pointing at those pages. Same question, completely different epistemics. One is a guess with good manners. The other is checkable.

Slide 12 · 6:18Who gives receipts in July 2026? Perplexity was built around this: every sentence carries a numbered source you can click. ChatGPT does it when search is on. Gemini has Deep Research, which browses dozens of pages and returns a fully cited report. The names and buttons will have changed by the time you watch this, they change monthly, and it does not matter, because the test never changes. Can you click through to where each claim came from? That is the receipts test. Apply it to any tool, this year or in five years.

Slide 13 · 6:55And here is the one sentence that upgrades any AI, even a plain chatbot. Say: answer with sources I can click, and if you cannot find a real source for a claim, say so instead of guessing. You are not casting a spell. You are setting an expectation, the same one you would set with a new human assistant: receipts matter more than fluency here. Grounded tools do this by default. Plain chatbots, at minimum, will start flagging what they are unsure of, and that flag is worth gold.

Slide 14 · 7:31Try this today, it takes one minute. Ask a plain chatbot: what did the 2026 Lancet analysis find about fabricated references? Then ask a grounded tool the same question. Watch the difference. One gives you a confident paragraph from memory. The other searches, then answers, with links you can open. Ask yourself which answer you could hand to a skeptical boss. That feeling, that difference, is the whole module. Ask tools that show their receipts.

8:01Module 3 · Verify: lateral reading

Slide 15 · 8:01Module three. Verify. Now it gets fun, because I get to teach you the single most valuable research habit of the decade, and it takes sixty seconds to use.

Slide 16 · 8:12First, why you need it. This year, Columbia Journalism Review ran a simple, brutal test. They took real excerpts from real news articles, handed them to eight AI search tools, and asked: where is this from? The tools gave wrong answers on more than sixty percent of the queries. Wrong article, wrong outlet, or a link that did not support the claim at all. And here is the detail that should stay with you: the wrong answers sounded exactly as confident as the right ones. Not one hedge, not one wobble. Confidence, it turns out, is a style setting, not a truth meter.

Slide 17 · 8:54So what do professionals do? Not what you would guess. When a fact-checker lands on an unfamiliar page or claim, they do not read it more carefully. They leave. They open a new tab and see what the rest of the web says about that claim and that source. Reading down the page means judging a source by its own self-presentation, its design, its About page, and anyone can buy a nice template. Reading across the web means letting independent sources judge it for you. It is called lateral reading, and it is not just more reliable than deep-reading the page. It is faster.

Slide 18 · 9:35The habit has a name you can remember: SIFT, from digital literacy researcher Mike Caulfield. Four moves, in order. Stop: before you share, cite, or believe, pause and ask if you actually know this source. Investigate the source: one new tab, thirty seconds, who is behind this? Find better coverage: search the claim itself and see if anyone reliable reports the same thing. Trace to the original: follow the quote or the statistic upstream to where it first appeared, because context changes everything. Stop. Investigate. Find. Trace. That is the whole toolkit.

Slide 19 · 10:12In practice, you will mostly use the sixty-second version. Take the one claim your decision rests on, the load-bearing claim. Open a new tab. Search it. Find one independent source that confirms it before you build on it. Independent matters: a second AI summarizing the same guess is not a source. And no, you do not check every sentence, that would be paranoia, not research. You check the load-bearing ones. The stat in your report. The quote in your post. The number you are about to act on.

Slide 20 · 10:47One more thing before the citation trap, and this is the rule that organizes everything. Never use the AI's confidence as your signal. Wrong answers and right answers sound identical, we just proved that. Use cost instead. If the answer is wrong and it costs you nothing, enjoy the speed and move on. If the answer is wrong and it costs you a grade, money, health, or your reputation, that answer gets the sixty-second check. Confidence is style. Cost is your policy.

11:20Module 4 · The citation trap

Slide 21 · 11:20Module four. The citation trap. This is the module I built the whole course around, because this is the one that catches smart, careful people.

Slide 22 · 11:30Let me give you the numbers as they stand this summer. On literature-review tasks, benchmark studies find frontier models still fabricate somewhere between eighteen and twenty-eight percent of the citations they produce. Roughly one in four or five. The very best models on the easiest tasks get down to a few percent, and on harder tasks they climb back toward one in five. The trend is improving, slowly. But no model yet reliably does the one thing an honest assistant would do, which is say: I could not find a source for that.

Slide 23 · 12:07Why does this happen? Because of what a language model fundamentally is. It learned, from millions of real papers, what a citation looks like: author names, a year, a journal, a volume, a DOI. A shape. And when it needs a citation it cannot retrieve, it does not go look one up. It completes the shape. Real researcher names from the right field, attached to a paper they never wrote. A real journal, with an invented article inside it. A DOI formatted perfectly that resolves to nothing, or worse, to a different paper. Nobody programmed deception. It is doing exactly what it was trained to do: produce plausible text. Plausible is the problem.

Slide 24 · 12:52So know your enemy. A fabricated citation has plausible authors, often real people from the right subfield. It has a real-sounding venue, an actual journal or a perfect imitation of one. It has a well-formed DOI, every hyphen in place. It has the right year, sensible page numbers, every convention followed. It is built, by the statistics of its training, to survive a glance. Which means the glance, the eyeball test you and I would naturally apply, is worthless. Let that one go. You will never out-stare a forgery that was optimized to pass staring.

Slide 25 · 13:30Which brings us to the only rule in this course I will state in stone. A citation you never opened is a citation you cannot trust. Not from the smartest model, not in the most professional formatting, not when nineteen of its neighbors checked out fine. If your work rests on it, you open it. That single habit, applied only to the references you actually rely on, catches essentially every fabricated citation ever produced.

Slide 26 · 14:00Opening it means the three-click check, and it takes about thirty seconds. Click one: does it exist? Paste the title into Google Scholar, or the DOI into your browser. If nothing comes back, you have your answer. Click two: is it what it claims to be? Right authors, right journal, right year? Fakes love to borrow a real title and swap the details. Click three: does it say what the AI said it says? Open the abstract. Because the subtlest failure is a real paper cited for a claim it never made. Exists. Matches. Says it. Three clicks.

Slide 27 · 14:39Your homework for this module, and I mean tonight: ask any chatbot for three sources on a niche topic you know well. Niche is the key word. Fakes cluster where the training data is thin, so a narrow question is where you will meet your first one. Then open all three. Not skim. Open. In the AI Lab, the Citation Hunt game will let you practice on a stack I prepared, where I promise the fakes are labeled honestly after you guess. Many people meet their first fabricated citation inside the next five minutes. It changes how you read forever.

15:19Module 5 · Deep research, and its blind spot

Slide 28 · 15:19Module five. Deep research. The newest, shiniest thing in AI research, and the place where everything from modules two, three, and four comes together.

Slide 29 · 15:29What these tools do is honestly spectacular. You ask one question, compare the evidence on X, what is the state of research on Y. The agent takes over your question the way a junior analyst would: it runs dozens of searches, opens dozens of pages, takes notes, cross-references, and hands you back a structured, cited report, pages long, in minutes. ChatGPT, Gemini, Claude, and Perplexity all ship a version of this in 2026. Work that used to take a day now takes a coffee break. Use these tools. I do.

Slide 30 · 16:05And read this before you trust one. The reports look finished. Clean sections, formal tone, numbered references, everything about them signals, done, audited, safe. The reference checks say otherwise. Studies this year keep finding the same pattern: inside otherwise-good reports sit unsupported claims and invented or misattributed sources. The failure hides beautifully, one weak source among nineteen real ones, wrapped in perfect formatting. The polish is the trap. A report that looks audited has not been audited. You are the audit.

Slide 31 · 16:37So here is the reframe that makes deep research safe: treat every report as a first draft with leads, assembled by a fast assistant who occasionally pads the bibliography. Use it for the map, the terrain, the players, the candidate sources. Do not outsource the conclusion. The workflow is generate, then audit. The AI does the breadth. You do the depth, exactly where it matters. People who skip the audit are not doing research with AI. They are doing vibes with citations.

Slide 32 · 17:10The audit itself is one habit. Find the load-bearing sources: the two or three references the report's main conclusion actually rests on. They are usually easy to spot, they sit under the headline numbers. Run the three-click check on those. Ten minutes, tops. If the foundation holds, you have a strong draft you can trust and refine. If even one foundation source is fabricated or misread, distrust the whole report, because you have just learned how carefully it was assembled. This one habit decides whether deep research makes you faster and right, or just faster.

17:48Module 6 · Stay honest, and when to trust

Slide 33 · 17:48Module six, the short one, and the one I most want you to keep. Everything so far was technique. This module is judgment, the part that stays yours.

Slide 34 · 17:59First, a promise about shelf life. Every number I have quoted this hour will change. Fabrication rates will fall, grounding will improve, new tools will ship next month with new names. Good. None of that touches what you learned, because receipts, lateral reading, opened citations, and load-bearing audits are not facts about models. They are habits about evidence. They worked before AI, they work on every model shipping today, and they will work on whatever ships in 2030. Judgment compounds. Models rotate.

Slide 35 · 18:32Second, honesty, in both directions. When AI helped you research or write something that matters, say so in the way the room expects. A class has a syllabus rule, a journal has a policy, a workplace has a norm, and they differ, so know your room, and when it is unclear, ask first. And two things hold in every room. Never present an unverified AI claim as your own checked work. And never send what you have not read. Disclosure costs one sentence. A fabricated citation with your name on it can cost a grade, a paper, or a reputation.

Slide 36 · 19:12Third, proportion. Not everything deserves the full protocol, and pretending otherwise would just mean you abandon the protocol by Thursday. So let cost decide. Low stakes, movie trivia, brainstorming, recipe ideas: trust freely, enjoy the speed. Medium stakes, a fact in an email, a purchase: sixty seconds on the one load-bearing claim. High stakes, money, health, grades, law, anything published with your name attached: grounded tools, lateral reading, every citation opened. You already know every move that list asks for.

Slide 37 · 19:44So, the whole course on one slide. Find well: ask tools that show receipts, retrieve-then-answer beats answer-from-memory. Verify well: read laterally, SIFT, one independent source for every load-bearing claim. Cite well: open every citation that matters, three clicks, no exceptions for confident tone. Judge well: deep research is a first draft, disclose when AI helped, and let cost set the depth of the check. Four lines. They will outlive every model name I said today.

When the answer arrives: find the load-bearing claim, new tab, one independent source. Before you cite: open it, exists, matches, says it. Before you ship: if a deep research report is involved, audit its foundation sources, then disclose what AI did. And always: confidence is style, receipts are evidence, cost decides how hard you check. Notice there is not one tool name in that list. Tools rotate. The checklist is permanent.
