Lecture transcript

The full narration of the Course 03 v2 lecture, Research with AI: the complete 2026 toolkit. Timings estimated at about 150 words per minute (roughly 20:35 total); they re-sync when the v2 lecture film premieres. Narrated by Aira, our AI teacher. Facts are a July 2026 snapshot.

0:00Welcome

Slide 1 · 0:00Welcome back to Cambium Academy. This is Course 3, Research with AI, version two: the complete 2026 toolkit. Not one chatbot used like a better search engine, but the whole instrument rack: agents that read the literature while you sleep, assistants that turn plain English into working analysis, machine eyes for your figures and field notes, a library that talks back, and the writing and disclosure rules that keep all of it honest. Ten instruments, ten modules, one certificate.

Slide 2 · 0:31This course is built for people who work with evidence: researchers, grad students, analysts, and anyone whose work rests on what is true. You need no code, though the analysis module goes deeper if you bring R or Python. A browser, about three hours, and zero dollars. And one realization to start: most researchers use one AI tool for everything. In 2026 that is like running a lab with only a hammer.

Slide 3 · 1:00Here is the deal. By the end you can send research agents to the literature and audit what they bring back. Design studies with a tireless methods consultant before data exists. Turn plain English into analysis you can defend. Give your papers machine eyes and ears. Write, translate, and pre-review with the actual policies of Science, Nature, Elsevier, and NIH straight. And you will leave with your personal stack: one page, your instrument for every job.

Slide 4 · 1:30The path is the Academy standard. Read the lecture, clear the twenty-four flashcards, win the five AI Lab games, then twenty questions, fourteen to pass, certificate on the spot. One tip before you start: keep your own current project in mind the whole way. Every module ends with a try-it aimed at your work, not a toy.

1:53Module 1 · The 2026 research stack

Slide 5 · 1:53Module one. Before any tools, the shape of the whole thing.

Slide 6 · 1:58Meet Kosmos, an autonomous AI scientist. In a single run it processes about fifteen hundred papers and writes forty-two thousand lines of analysis code. Its own makers estimate eighty percent of its findings hold up under expert review. Read that both ways. Six months of work in a day. And one finding in five, wrong. That is the bargain every AI instrument now offers you, and both halves are the point.

Slide 7 · 2:26The rack has seven instrument classes. Discover: agents that search, read, and synthesize literature. Design: a methods consultant before data exists. Analyze: plain English to working code. See and hear: machine eyes for figures and notebooks, a talking library. Write: drafts, translation, review. Reproduce and disclose: documentation, real references, the paper trail. One chatbot is not a research strategy; knowing which instrument each job wants is.

Slide 8 · 2:52And the sentence that governs everything: AI supplies speed. You supply trust. Speed is now nearly free. Trust never was, and never will be. Every module from here hands you one instrument, one measured fact about where it fails, and the one check that makes it safe. The rack changes monthly. The rule does not.

Slide 9 · 3:14Try this now. List last week's research tasks and tag each with its instrument class. Find the two you still do fully by hand. Those two are where this course pays for itself first. Keep the list: module ten turns it into your personal stack, and that page is your capstone.

3:35Module 2 · Prompting for researchers

Slide 10 · 3:35Module two. The foundation everything else uses, so it comes first, not last.

Slide 11 · 3:40The recipe has four parts. Context: what the machine cannot guess, your field, your data, your constraints, what good looks like. Deliverable: a job, not a topic. Format: the exact shape, length, structure, style. And the researcher's upgrade, demanded uncertainty: flag what you are not sure of and what I must verify. That fourth line converts a confident narrator into an honest assistant, and it costs you nothing.

Slide 12 · 4:07The standing sentence deserves its own slide. Flag what you are unsure of, state the assumptions you just made, and tell me what to verify before relying on this. Why does it work? Models are tuned to be helpful and fluent; an empty answer feels like failure to them, so they fill gaps. This sentence gives permission to show the seams. Paste it into every factual request for the rest of your career.

Slide 13 · 4:36Four patterns that earn their keep. Act as reviewer 2: attack this draft, in private, before humans do. List the assumptions you just made: the list is where the errors live. Give the opposing interpretation: forces the model off your side, where you are blindest. And the most scientific sentence you can say to a machine: what would falsify this?

Slide 14 · 5:00One carry-over from Course 2, because the evidence still holds: personas shape tone, not truth. Telling the model it is a world-class statistician changes the costume, and measured accuracy, slightly for the worse. For facts and statistics: plain ask, full context, demanded uncertainty. Save the roles for voice.

Slide 15 · 5:20Try it now. Find this week's most disappointing AI answer. Rerun it with the full recipe. The difference between the two answers is the skill, and it takes about two minutes to apply.

5:33Module 3 · Discover: the literature pipeline

Slide 16 · 5:33Module three. Discovery, as a pipeline, not a search box.

Slide 17 · 5:37Four gears, in order of effort. Gear one, search-grounded Q and A: a quick cited answer with receipts you can click. Gear two, citation maps: the field's neighborhood around one seed paper. Gear three, deep research agents: a synthesized, cited report while you do something better with your time. Gear four, standing alerts: the review that keeps updating itself. Most researchers only ever use gear one, and use it like Google.

Slide 18 · 6:05Gear two, the maps. Connected Papers: one seed paper in, a visual similarity graph out, including work that never cites each other. ResearchRabbit: chain recommendations interactively and set alerts as collections grow. Litmaps: a living map over two hundred sixty million papers. The payoff is the adjacent method community you did not know existed.

Slide 19 · 6:27Gear three, the agents, measured. OpenAI Deep Research: fifty to two hundred sources in ten to thirty minutes. Gemini: thirty to one fifty. Claude: fewer sources, deeper synthesis. Perplexity: a free tier exists. Every major AI ships one now. You write the question; it plans, searches, reads, cross-references, and writes a cited report.

Slide 20 · 6:48But for literature specifically, the specialists win. Elicit and Undermind search closed, verified databases of published research, so their citations resolve by construction, and they speak researcher: screening columns, effect extraction, exportable tables. Rule of thumb: literature review means academic agent first, generalist second. Everything else, the reverse.

Slide 21 · 7:07The rule that makes gear three safe: every agent report is a first draft with leads, assembled by a fast assistant who occasionally pads the bibliography. The polish signals audited. Nothing was audited. Find the two or three sources the conclusion rests on and run the three-click check on those. Then set the alert so the answer stays current.

Slide 22 · 7:31Try it now, the two-agent shootout. One question from your actual research, through one generalist agent and through Elicit. Count the sources that survive a click: exists, right venue, actually says it. The difference is the lesson.

7:46Module 4 · Trust: the citation trap

Slide 23 · 7:46Module four. The module that made version one of this course, condensed to its spine, because the numbers got worse, not better.

Slide 24 · 7:54Four numbers. One in two hundred seventy-seven papers now carries a fabricated reference, up roughly six-fold in three years, from a Lancet analysis of two million papers. AI search tools were wrong on more than sixty percent of citation queries in Columbia Journalism Review's test, in a perfectly confident tone. Frontier models still fabricate eighteen to twenty-eight percent of citations on literature tasks. And three clicks is all it takes to catch essentially every fake.

Slide 25 · 8:24Why the fakes pass a glance: models learned what a citation looks like. Plausible authors, a real-sounding journal, a well-formed DOI, sensible pages. When unsure, the model completes the pattern; no lookup happens. Nobody programmed deception. Plausible text is the product, and that is exactly the problem. The eyeball test is worthless by construction.

Slide 26 · 8:46The verification kit: read across, not down. Leave the page and let independent sources judge it. SIFT: Stop. Investigate the source. Find better coverage. Trace to the original. Faster and more reliable than staring harder at the thing you are trying to judge.

Slide 27 · 9:03And the one rule this course carves in stone: a citation you never opened is a citation you cannot trust. Click one: does it exist? Click two: is it what it claims, right authors, right venue, right year? Click three: does it actually say what the AI said it says? Thirty seconds per citation that matters.

Slide 28 · 9:26Try it now: the Lab's first three games, Citation Hunt, Lateral Race, and Receipts-or-Vibes, train exactly this module. Then ask any chatbot for three sources on a niche topic you know well, and open all three. Many researchers meet their first fabricated citation within five minutes. It changes how you read forever.

9:46Module 5 · Design before data

Slide 29 · 9:46Module five. The cheapest AI hour in your whole project, and the one almost nobody uses.

Slide 30 · 9:53Before any data exists, the machine is a tireless methods consultant. Power analysis and sample size, sanity-checked before a season of fieldwork is committed to an underpowered design. Test selection with assumptions stated. Confound hunting: list what a hostile reviewer would raise. Pre-registration drafting, while your hypotheses are still honest. Design errors are silent and permanent; no clever analysis rescues a broken design.

Slide 31 · 10:18The consulting rule: show the math or it did not happen. Demand the formula and every assumption behind the number, then ask what changes if each assumption is wrong. Cross-check with a second tool; two independent calculations that agree beat ten confident ones. And when stakes are high, the human statistician still signs. The AI chose the test is not a methods section.

Slide 32 · 10:43Try it now. Describe your next study in six sentences. Ask for the three most likely fatal design flaws, and the sample size math shown in full. Then ask what design a hostile reviewer would propose instead. You do not have to agree. You have to have heard it before they say it.

11:04Module 6 · Your AI data analyst

Slide 33 · 11:04Module six. Analysis: this is not autocomplete anymore.

Slide 34 · 11:08What changed: assistants can see. Posit Assistant, inside Positron and RStudio, reads your dataframe, your code, and your rendered plot at once, and reasons across R and Python in the same analysis. Agentic assistants navigate whole repositories. Browser analysts run plain-English analyses on uploaded data. Even MATLAB ships its own AI chat. The interface became a colleague who can see your screen.

Slide 35 · 11:32So the safety rules become a loop. Describe data, goal, and constraints. Require commented code. The explain-it-back rule: every line explained before anything runs. The tiny-test rule: ten rows you can check by hand, then scale. And keep the transcript; the conversation is your analysis log. The silent failures, plausible-but-wrong tests, hallucinated arguments, unit errors, all die at steps three and four.

Slide 36 · 11:57And the lane nobody builds for: qualitative research. Whisper-class transcription turns interview hours into text in minutes. Thematic coding: the AI proposes, you adjudicate, and the audit trail of accepted and rejected codes is what makes it a methodology instead of a shortcut. Survey open-ends, clustered and quantified in an afternoon.

Slide 37 · 12:18Try it now: hand an assistant your messiest real CSV with a plain-English description of every column. Ask for cleaning plus one labeled plot, every step commented and explained back. Run the tiny test first. Then the full file.

12:33Module 7 · See and hear

Slide 38 · 12:33Module seven. Machine eyes for your material, and a voice for your library.

Slide 39 · 12:38What the eyes can do: extract numbers from published charts, including that 1998 figure nobody has data for. First-pass reads of micrographs and site maps. Transcription of handwritten field notebooks, including handwriting you can barely read yourself. Tables pulled out of photos. The eternal scanned-appendix problem, mostly solved.

Slide 40 · 12:58And the measured limit: vision models still misidentify fine-grained structures in cluttered scientific imagery that trained experts catch. Treat them as brilliant interns with astigmatism. Two rules make them safe. Every extracted number gets spot-verified against the source. And every identification is a hypothesis for the expert, never a result for the paper.

Slide 41 · 13:19Now the ears. Source-grounded library assistants, the NotebookLM class: upload your papers and ask questions; answers ground in your corpus and cite back to the page. Audio overviews turn your sources into a podcast-style discussion you can interrupt mid-sentence with your own questions. Maps, decks, and quizzes on demand. The library becomes study materials while you commute.

Slide 42 · 13:42Why grounding matters: containment, not elimination. An open chatbot free-associates from everything it ever read, so checking its errors means searching the world. A grounded assistant errs inside your uploads, with citations that point at pages you own. Same receipts principle as module three, applied to your own bookshelf. Try it tonight: three papers in, one critical-format overview, and interrupt it with the question you would ask the authors.

14:09Module 8 · Write, translate, survive review

Slide 43 · 14:09Module eight. Writing: the most-used AI research task on earth, and the most policed.

Slide 44 · 14:15The legitimate power tools. Revision and clarity passes: your ideas, better sentences. Translation and English polishing, the great equalizer for non-native speakers, and at many venues it now counts as exempt copy-editing, but check yours. The unglamorous fleet: abstracts, cover letters, plain-language summaries, response-to-reviewers scaffolds. And grant support: aims critiqued, reviewer simulated, boilerplate drafted, while the science stays yours.

Slide 45 · 14:38The policy table, July 2026, because the rules now differ for real. Science: disclosure of all AI use, including copy editing, in the acknowledgments. The Nature portfolio: copy-editing exempt, AI-generated images banned, substantive use disclosed in methods. Elsevier: a dedicated declaration section. NIH: reviewers barred from feeding applications to AI, and proposals substantially written by AI can be rejected. One-size-fits-all advice is now wrong by construction: check the venue's page at submission, every time.

Slide 46 · 15:08Pre-review yourself. Attack this manuscript as a skeptical reviewer: clarity, methods gaps, statistical red flags, overclaimed conclusions, missing limitations. It is tireless and shameless, which is exactly what you want in private, before submission. And know that the machines are already on the other side: roughly twenty-one percent of reviews at a major AI conference were flagged as substantially AI-written. Write for humans; expect machines to read you first.

Slide 47 · 15:36The red lines. Never upload someone else's unpublished manuscript or grant to an AI tool; what you review is confidential, and NIH bars it explicitly. Never let AI invent citations, data, or quotes; module four showed you how convincingly it will. And never skip the venue's declaration: disclosure costs a sentence, discovery costs a reputation. Tonight: paste your own abstract, ask for the three harshest justified referee comments, fix one before bed.

16:04Module 9 · Reproducible and intact

Slide 48 · 16:04Module nine. The two quiet crises, and the assistant that fixes both, if you ask.

Slide 49 · 16:10Crisis one: analyses nobody can rerun. Undocumented scripts, unpinned environments, the folder called final underscore v2 underscore REAL. Crisis two: reference lists nobody checked; module four measured that one. The fix for both costs one sentence each, because the same assistant that wrote your analysis will document it, pin it, script it, and write the README. Most people never ask. Reproducibility went from a virtue to a prompt.

Slide 50 · 16:38The four asks, for code you already have: document every function and non-obvious line. Pin this environment, with versions. Turn this interactive session into a rerunnable script. Write the README a stranger could follow. AI drafts, you correct, and the corrections are exactly where your expertise belongs.

Slide 51 · 16:56Reference integrity: the two nevers. Never hand-type a citation, and never trust an AI-typed one. Records come from the source of record: Zotero pulls from Crossref and publishers, Better BibTeX keeps keys stable, and AI plugins are a reading layer over your library, never a source of bibliographic truth. The final gate before submission: every DOI resolves. Minutes, not hours.

Slide 52 · 17:20Try both tonight: have AI document one existing script, then correct its two wrong guesses. And run your reference list through a DOI check. Almost every list has at least one casualty; better you find it than reviewer two.

17:36Module 10 · Responsible AI, and your stack

Slide 53 · 17:36Module ten. The capstone: judgment, written down.

Slide 54 · 17:39The gap where careers get hurt, measured. About seventy percent of journals now have an explicit AI policy. In one analysis of seventy-five thousand recent papers, about zero point one percent disclosed AI use. That gap closes on the disclosers' terms, and proper disclosure usually costs one sentence. Be early.

Slide 55 · 17:59The working code, five lines. Verify before you rely: you now own every check. Disclose per the venue's actual rule. Guard sensitive data absolutely: confidential, pre-publication, and human-subjects data never touch a cloud chatbot, and local or institution-hosted models exist exactly for that lane. Watch the bias: training data is not the world; ask what is missing. And you remain the accountable author. Every instrument assists. None of them signs.

Slide 56 · 18:27One line in that code is hard enough to repeat: if your data could not go in an email to a stranger, it does not go in a consumer chatbot. When in doubt: local model, institutional deployment, or synthetic stand-in data. This is the lane the Academy's local-models course will cover hands-on.

Slide 57 · 18:48And the capstone. Build your personal stack, one page. Your discovery agent and map tool. Your methods consult and coding assistant. Your vision tool, library assistant, and writing setup. Your documentation habit and reference pipeline. Your venue's disclosure rule and your data red lines, written down. Free tiers marked, so the stack survives a zero-dollar month. The tools on the page will rotate. The shape of it will not. That page is the deliverable this certificate stands behind.
