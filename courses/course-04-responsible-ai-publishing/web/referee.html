◈ THE COURSE'S OWN RULE, MADE REAL

THE LOCAL REFEREE

Module 8 told you never to put a confidential manuscript into a cloud AI system. So this page does not have one. It downloads a language model into your browser and runs it on your own graphics card. After the model loads, you can pull out your network cable and it still works. Paste the manuscript you are actually reviewing. It cannot leave this machine.

How to verify this yourself, in thirty seconds, without trusting us:
1. Load the model below and wait for it to finish.
2. Open your browser's DevTools (F12) and select the Network tab. Clear it.
3. Go offline: DevTools has an Offline throttling preset, or simply disconnect your wifi.
4. Paste a manuscript and run the referee. It answers anyway, and the Network tab stays empty.
That is what a sanctioned tool looks like. It is not a promise; it is an architecture.
Two things we did on purpose, because a page that promises zero network has to mean it:
· This page carries no analytics. Every other page in the Academy has a privacy-friendly visitor counter on it. This one does not, because it would make a request, and the counter above would have to report it. We would rather lose the pageview than the point.
· If you open Aira, the AI teacher in the corner, and ask her something, the counter WILL tick up. That is not a bug, and we did not hide it. Aira is a cloud AI: your question goes to a server. The referee is a local one: your manuscript does not. This page will show you the difference in real time, on the same screen, which is the whole lesson in one number.
STEP 1 · LOAD THE MODEL

Bring the referee into your browser

A small open-weights model is downloaded once from its public host and cached. That download is the only network activity this page ever makes. After it completes, everything is local, forever, offline included.

STEP 2 · PUT IT TO WORK

Three jobs, all of them inside the rules

Referee a confidential manuscript
The job you may never send to a cloud AI. Paste the manuscript you are reviewing and get a red-flag pass: contradictions, unsupported claims, statistical impossibilities, missing controls.
Draft my disclosure statement
Describe what you actually did with AI, and get a statement in the shape your venue expects, with the responsibility sentence intact.
Decode a reviewer comment
Paste the cryptic criticism you cannot parse, and get the possible readings, so you answer the one they actually meant.
What this referee will never do: it will not tell you whether a text was written by AI, and it will refuse if you ask. That is not a technical limit; it is the course's rule in stone, built into the system prompt. A detector score is not evidence, and a small language model guessing at authorship is worse than a detector. It hunts bad science instead, which is what a referee is for.
WHY THIS PAGE EXISTS

The medium is the argument

Every policy in Module 8 says the same thing: the manuscript is not yours to share. NIH prohibits generative AI in peer review outright. CVPR bans LLM-written reviews. NeurIPS bars sharing submissions. And every one of those rules leaves an honest reviewer stuck, because the help would genuinely be useful and the only tools on offer are somebody else's servers.

This page is the third answer. Not "use the cloud AI and hope," and not "give up the help." Instead: bring the model to the manuscript, instead of sending the manuscript to the model. Confidentiality is preserved not by a promise in a privacy policy, but by the fact that no request is ever made.

When the policies talk about "an approved secure tool," this is the shape of the thing they mean. You just used one, and you were able to check it yourself.

Still your responsibility: your venue's rules govern, not this page. Some venues bar AI assistance in review entirely, however local it is. Read the policy, and disclose the help you used. The Author's Pack has a reviewer disclosure line ready to adapt.
