The Poisoned Manuscript

This page looks clean. Somewhere on it, invisible to your eyes, is an instruction meant for an AI reviewer, planted exactly the way the July 2025 arXiv preprints did it. Use the forensic tools, find where the poison hides, and identify what it says.

Human eyes read the rendered page. Machines read the text layer. The difference is the whole attack.

Effects of Canopy Thinning on Understory Bird Diversity: A Three-Season Field Study

Submitted to Meridian · manuscript 2417-R0 · CONFIDENTIAL, for review purposes only

Forest management increasingly balances timber production against biodiversity goals. We surveyed understory bird communities across 24 thinned and unthinned stands over three breeding seasons, using standardized point counts and mist-net captures.

Methods

Paired stands were matched on elevation, aspect, and stand age. Detection-corrected abundance was modeled with N-mixture models; species richness used rarefaction to control survey effort.

Results

Thinned stands showed higher understory richness (mean +3.1 species, 95% CI 1.8 to 4.4), driven by shrub-nesting insectivores. Two canopy-dependent species declined. Effects stabilized by the third season.

Discussion

Moderate thinning appears compatible with, and partly beneficial for, understory bird diversity, with the important exception of canopy specialists, for which we recommend retention patches.

References
1. MacArthur, R. H., & MacArthur, J. W. (1961). On bird species diversity. Ecology, 42(3), 594–598.
2. Royle, J. A. (2004). N-mixture models for estimating population size from spatially replicated counts. Biometrics, 60(1), 108–115.
