The capstone

Three artifacts you would really sign. Not an exercise: a disclosure statement for your next paper, a one-page AI policy for your lab or journal, and an honest audit of a paper you have already published. Do these once and you will use them for years.

1Your disclosure statement

Write the statement you will attach to your next submission, for the venue you will really send it to.

What it must contain

  • The tool and version, named plainly.
  • What it actually did. Specific enough to be true, general enough to survive minor edits.
  • What the humans did after. Reviewed and edited all output, and take full responsibility for the content.
  • The right section. Look up your venue's current policy page today; do not rely on this course, or on a colleague's memory of last year's rule.
The tiebreak for edge cases: disclose anything a careful reader would feel misled to discover later; skip anything they would find trivial. That sentence resolves cases the policies have not caught up with yet, and it always lands on the defensible side.

2Your one-page AI policy

For yourself, your lab, or your journal. One page, written in your own words, that a new student or a new reviewer could follow on their first day.

The six things it should settle

  • Permitted uses. Name them generously and concretely: language and translation, restructuring, brainstorming, consistency checks, code assistance with verification. Be the person who says what IS allowed, not just what is not.
  • Disclosure defaults. What your group discloses by default, and where, so nobody has to guess at midnight.
  • The verification rule. Every citation opened. Every number checked. Every generated line of analysis explained back before it runs.
  • The confidentiality line. Manuscripts you are reviewing, unpublished data, and anything under embargo never enter an AI system, unless the venue explicitly provides a sanctioned one.
  • The images rule. No generative images of data, ever. Schematics only with disclosure.
  • Who signs. Who is accountable for a submission's AI use, and what happens if something is discovered later. The answer is always a human, and the policy should name the role.
Write the detector clause too. If you supervise, teach, or edit, put it in writing: no one in this group will be accused on the basis of an AI-detector score. That sentence protects the people who most need protecting, and it costs you nothing but the courage to write it down.

3The honest audit

Take one paper you have already published, ideally a recent one, and run this course's checks on it. Whatever you find, you will know, and knowing is the point.

The audit

  • Every DOI in the reference list resolves. Click them. All of them. Count the casualties.
  • The load-bearing citations pass three clicks: exists, matches, and actually says what you attached to it.
  • Tables, figures, and text agree on every number that appears in more than one place.
  • The statistics are internally consistent: do the test statistics, degrees of freedom, and p-values fit together?
  • The AI use, if any, is disclosed correctly for that venue's rule as it stood at submission.

If you find something, you have three honest options depending on severity: note it in your own records, contact the journal, or publish a correction. Every one of those is better than the fourth option, which is hoping the Integrity Hub does not get there first.

Most people find something. A dead DOI, a rounding mismatch, a citation that says a little less than they claimed. That is not a scandal; that is what an unaudited reference list looks like, which is exactly why this course exists.

The rubric, if you want to grade yourself

CriterionWhat good looks like
Correct placementThe disclosure sits in the section YOUR venue's current policy page specifies, and you can point to the page.
Honest scopeIt names substantive use and skips trivia. Neither vague nor a tool inventory.
Responsibility claimedThe statement says a human reviewed the output and is accountable for the content.
Policy is usableA new student could follow your one-pager on day one without asking you a question.
Policy is generousIt says clearly what IS permitted, not only what is forbidden. Honest people should feel safer after reading it.
The detector clauseWritten down: nobody gets accused on a score.
Audit actually runEvery DOI clicked, not sampled. The count of what you found is written down somewhere.
Action takenIf the audit found something material, you did something about it.

Then show it, if you want

Post your one-page policy on the community board. Policies get better when other people poke at them, and the next person writing one will be glad yours is there.
