The course · three chairs

Publish, review, and edit with AI in the room

Course 04's thesis, defended by all twelve modules: the question is no longer whether AI was used. Nearly everyone uses it, and detectors cannot reliably tell. The answerable questions are better ones: is the science sound, are the data authentic, are the conclusions supported, and was the use disclosed?

  • The author's chair: AI is never an author; disclose per your venue's actual rule (acknowledgments for writing help, methods for research use), and own the irreducible seven.
  • Where AI honestly helps: brainstorming, language and translation, restructuring, consistency checks, and citations you verify yourself. Taught generously, without guilt.
  • What crosses the line: fabricated references (1 in 277 biomedical papers by early 2026), invented data, manipulated figures, ghost-written reviews, citation padding.
  • The reviewer's chair: confidentiality first. Never upload a manuscript you are reviewing. Then hunt bad science, not AI fingerprints.
  • The editor's chair: screening tools support decisions, they never make them. And a detector score is never, ever the basis for accusing anyone.
