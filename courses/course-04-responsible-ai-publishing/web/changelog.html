Responsible AI in Scholarly Publishing, first edition

  • Twelve modules across the three chairs you will actually sit in: author (disclosure, honest uses, the irreducible seven), reviewer (the confidentiality bright line, and hunting bad science instead of AI), and editor (screening tools that support decisions without making them).
  • Every policy and statistic re-verified live on 13 July 2026 against the primary source. Several widely repeated figures turned out to be stale or misattributed, and this course carries the corrected versions with their dates.
  • AI Lab of six games, including Ghost Citations, which grades your calls against the live Crossref registry from your own browser, and The Detector Trap, which can only be won by refusing to accuse.
  • The Practical (honors exam): one editor's packet, four tasks, and a decision letter that automatically fails if it accuses the authors on an AI-detector score.
  • The thesis, defended in every module: the question is no longer whether AI was used. It is whether the science is sound, the data authentic, the conclusions supported, and the use disclosed.
Why a changelog on a free course? Because this course tells you to distrust confident, undated claims, and it would be absurd to be one. Every policy and number in this course is a dated fact with a source on the resources page. When a policy is revised, a rate changes, or a tool dies, the fix lands here with the date attached. If you spot something stale before we do, say so on the community board: that is not a complaint, it is the course working.
