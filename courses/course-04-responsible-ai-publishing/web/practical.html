Antimicrobial peptide gradients predict biofilm persistence in clinical catheter isolates

Corresponding author: Institute of Microbiology, Universidade Federal (submitted in English, authors' second language) · CONFIDENTIAL, for editorial use only

Catheter-associated infections remain a leading cause of hospital-acquired morbidity. We characterized biofilm persistence across 46 clinical isolates and tested whether antimicrobial peptide (AMP) gradients predict persistence phenotypes.

Methods (excerpt)

Isolates were cultured under standardized flow conditions. Persistence was scored by residual biomass after 24 h challenge. Gradient response curves were fitted using a four-parameter logistic model in R.

Results (excerpt)

AMP gradient steepness predicted persistence (R² = 0.62). Twelve isolates showed complete tolerance. Effect held after adjusting for species and catheter material.

Statement on AI use

The authors used a generative AI assistant for language editing and to draft plotting code, reviewed all output, and take full responsibility for the content of this manuscript.

PART D · THE DECISION LETTER

Now write it

Write the decision letter to the authors of 3120-R0. Ground every request in evidence you can point to. Aira grades it against the course's standard, and there is one automatic failure: a letter that accuses the authors on the strength of the detector score fails, no matter how well it is written.

A strong letter usually: states the decision; names the fabricated reference specifically; asks about the hidden text neutrally, as a request for explanation rather than a verdict; raises the checkable scientific issues; and says nothing about the AI-detector score, because it is not evidence.
