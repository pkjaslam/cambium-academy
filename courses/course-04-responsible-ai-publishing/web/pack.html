The Author's Pack

The course, in a form you can use at 11pm the night before submission. A disclosure statement builder, ready statements per venue family, the reviewer's bright-line card, the editor's evidence rack, and the policy pages worth bookmarking. Copy any of it, or print the whole thing. Every rule here was verified on 13 July 2026, and every rule here should be re-checked at your venue before you submit.

1 · The disclosure statement builder

Pick your venue family and what you actually did. The statement below writes itself, in the right section, with the responsibility sentence intact.

Then do the one thing this builder cannot do for you: open your journal's own AI policy page and confirm the section. These rules changed in January and June of 2026 alone. Five minutes, every submission.

2 · The pre-submission checklist

  • Venue policy page opened today. Not last year's memory, not a colleague's summary.
  • Disclosure written and placed in the section that venue names.
  • Every DOI in the reference list resolves. Click all of them. This is the 1-in-277 check.
  • Load-bearing citations pass three clicks: exists, matches, says what you claim.
  • No generative images of data. Schematics only, disclosed. Original image files kept.
  • Tables, figures, and text agree on every number that appears twice.
  • The irreducible seven are yours: accuracy, interpretation, statistical conclusions, novelty claims, citations, ethics, and the signature.

3 · The reviewer's bright-line card

Never: upload, paste, or otherwise feed a confidential manuscript, its figures, its code, or your draft critique of it into any AI system, unless the venue explicitly provides a sanctioned tool with confidentiality assured. NIH prohibits generative AI in peer review outright; CVPR 2026 bans LLM-written reviews; NeurIPS bars sharing submissions. The manuscript is someone else's unpublished work, and confidentiality is a promise you personally made.
Legitimate, inside policy: learning an unfamiliar method or statistic in general terms (no manuscript text); walking a public reporting checklist (CONSORT, PRISMA, STROBE, ARRIVE); polishing your own review prose where the venue permits it, with disclosure; and drafting questions from your own reading notes.

Hunt bad science, not AI. The red flags:

  • Contradictions between sections (the abstract promises what the results never show).
  • Claims the cited reference does not actually support. Open the citation on the boldest sentence.
  • Unverifiable or fabricated citations: dead DOIs, papers no database has heard of.
  • Statistical impossibilities: a standard deviation of zero in noisy data, p-values that do not match their test statistics, means outside the possible range.
  • Methods that could not produce the reported results: sample sizes that do not match the tables.
  • Hallucinated software packages, datasets, or equations. Thirty seconds of searching settles each.
  • Figures that contradict the text; conclusions far broader than the data.
And never: recommend a decision because a paper "feels AI-written." That is not a finding, it cannot be answered by the authors, and it will not survive an appeal.

4 · The editor's evidence rack

Run this instead of reading a detector score. Every row is evidence about the work; none of it cares who typed.

CheckWhat it catches
Reference resolutionFabricated and mismatched citations. The single highest-yield check at intake.
Image forensicsDuplication, manipulation, and AI-generated figures (Proofig, Imagetwin class tools).
Statistical consistencyImpossible values, mismatched test statistics, sample sizes that do not add up.
Text-layer inspectionHidden prompt injection. Select-all is free and takes three seconds.
Duplicate and mill signalsThe same manuscript at six venues; known paper-mill fingerprints (Papermill Alarm traffic lights, STM Integrity Hub).
Data and code availabilityWhether the claims can be examined at all.
The rule in stone: an AI-detector score must never be the sole basis for accepting, rejecting, or accusing anyone. Detectors misclassified 61.3% of non-native English speakers' essays as AI-generated while scoring near-perfectly on native speakers, and a light edit defeats them entirely. A flag is a question. COPE flowcharts govern suspicion: gather evidence, contact the authors, let them respond, escalate to institutions only if warranted. Journals judge papers; institutions judge people.

5 · Statements you can adapt right now

Elsevier family: Declaration of generative AI and AI-assisted technologies in the writing process. During the preparation of this work the authors used [tool, version] to improve language and readability. The authors reviewed and edited the content as needed and take full responsibility for the content of the publication.
Nature portfolio (methods): [Tool, version] was used to assist with restructuring author-written text and generating draft analysis code, which the authors verified against the data. Use of AI for copy editing of author-written text is not treated as disclosable under journal policy.
ICMJE / medical (acknowledgments + methods): Acknowledgments: the authors used [tool, version] for language editing. Methods: [tool, version] assisted in generating statistical analysis code, which was reviewed and validated by the authors. Both uses were also stated in the cover letter at submission.
Reviewer disclosure (where permitted): I used [tool, version] to improve the readability of my own review text. No part of the manuscript, its figures, or its data was entered into any AI system, and all evaluative judgments in this review are my own.
Lab policy, detector clause: No member of this group will be accused of AI misuse on the basis of an AI-detection score. Concerns about a manuscript must be grounded in checkable evidence: unresolvable citations, image irregularities, statistical inconsistencies, or data that cannot be produced.
