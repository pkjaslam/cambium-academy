Put the certificate to work

This course certifies something specific and increasingly rare: you can use AI in publishing without endangering the record, and you can tell the difference between evidence and a vibe. Here is how to say that out loud.

Resume and CV lines

Pick the one that fits the chair you are applying for. Edit them into your own voice; do not paste them stiff.

Certified in Responsible AI in Scholarly Publishing (Cambium AI Research Institution, 2026). Applies the 2026 ICMJE, COPE, and publisher disclosure rules; audits reference lists against the Crossref registry; and evaluates manuscripts on evidence rather than AI-detection scores.
Peer reviewer with formal training in AI-era review integrity: maintains manuscript confidentiality under NIH and conference policy, screens for fabricated citations and statistical inconsistencies, and writes evidence-grounded review reports.
Editorial screening: reference verification, image-forensics workflows, and paper-mill signal triage, applied under COPE's misconduct procedures, with decisions documented on evidence and never on AI-detector output.
Wrote and implemented a lab AI-use policy covering permitted uses, disclosure defaults, confidentiality boundaries, and verification requirements for all AI-assisted manuscripts.

The questions you will actually be asked

By search committees, editors-in-chief, integrity officers, and increasingly by PIs hiring postdocs. Rehearse these; the answers are all in the course.

"How do you use AI in your writing, and how do you disclose it?"
Name your real uses without apology (language, restructuring, code assistance, consistency checks), say that you verify every output, and show that you know disclosure is venue-specific: acknowledgments for writing help, methods for research use, a declaration section at Elsevier. The tell of a serious answer is that you check the venue's page every submission.
"A detector flags a submission at 95%. What do you do?"
Set the score aside; it is not evidence. Detectors misclassify non-native English writing at extraordinary rates, and a light edit defeats them, so they fail in both directions. Run the evidence rack instead: do the references resolve, do the images pass forensics, are the statistics consistent, are the data available? Act on findings, never on a score.
"Do you use AI when you review?"
The confidential manuscript never goes into an AI system. That is the bright line, and NIH prohibits it outright. What is legitimate: learning an unfamiliar method in general terms, walking a reporting checklist, and polishing your own review text where the venue permits it with disclosure. Any answer that starts with "well, just for summarizing" fails this question.
"How would you catch a fabricated citation?"
Resolve every DOI, then three-click the load-bearing ones: it exists, the metadata match, and it says what the sentence claims. Roughly 1 in 277 biomedical papers carried a phantom reference by early 2026, so this is now a routine check, not a suspicion. Say that you run it on your own manuscripts before submission, and you have answered the question they were really asking.
"Where do you think this is going?"
Toward disclosed, bounded AI assistance under human oversight (the NeurIPS 2026 trial and the AAAI labeled-review pilot), automated verification of references, images, and statistics at intake, and one shared disclosure standard being drafted now by STM, COPE, and partners. The skills stay the same; the form fields catch up.

Where this course actually pays

  • Getting published without drama. A clean disclosure and a verified reference list are the cheapest insurance in your career, and both take minutes.
  • Being asked to review, and being asked again. Editors remember the referee who found the impossible statistic, not the one who returned two fluent paragraphs.
  • Editorial and integrity roles. Journals are staffing integrity work right now, and the qualification is exactly this: knowing which tools mean something and refusing to let any of them make the decision.
  • Protecting your students. If you supervise, you will one day be the person deciding whether a detector score becomes an accusation. Knowing the evidence in Module 7 is what stands between a careful student and a misconduct hearing.
