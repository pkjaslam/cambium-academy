The questions you will actually be asked

By hiring managers, technical interviewers, and the colleague who has to trust your automation. Rehearse these; the answers are all in the course.

"How do you decide whether a task should be automated by an agent?"
Score it against four marks: repetitive, verifiable, bounded, and low blast radius. If it is irreversible, high-stakes, or unverifiable, it goes on the do-not-automate list or keeps a human in the loop. And the honest test: if I would re-check every output anyway, automating it saved nothing and added a risk.
"How do you defend an agent against prompt injection?"
You do not do it with a prompt. The attacker's instruction sits right beside yours and the model cannot reliably tell them apart, so telling it to "ignore injections" fails. The defense is architecture: least privilege so a fooled agent has no dangerous tool, breaking the lethal trifecta (private data, untrusted content, external communication), and a human gate before anything irreversible. EchoLeak in 2025 is the case study.
"How do you know your agent actually works?"
A golden set: ten to thirty real cases, including the ugly ones, with known-good answers and pass/fail criteria decided in advance. I score the whole set, not a lucky example, and I rerun it on every change and on a schedule, because green today is not green tomorrow. About half of teams ship with no offline evals; that is the gap I close.
"An agent needs to send emails. How do you wire it?"
Carefully, because sending is irreversible and reaches real people. First choice: it drafts to a review folder and a human sends. If it must auto-send, a human approves each one, or it is scoped to a tiny, safe set of recipients and messages. What I never do is hand an agent an unrestricted send tool with no gate; that is the configuration behind the worst 2026 incidents.
"Where do you think this is going?"
Toward more capable agents and, honestly, more failures, because the capability outpaces the discipline. Gartner expects 40% of enterprise apps to use agents by end of 2026 and over 40% of agentic projects to be cancelled by 2027. The durable skill is not chasing the newest model; it is the boring craft of scoping, testing, and supervising. That is what stays valuable.

Where this course actually pays

  • Shipping automations that last. Anyone can demo an agent. The person who can make one still work next month, and prove it, is the one who gets trusted with the next project.
  • Being handed the risky ones. When a team needs an agent that touches money, customers, or production, they want the person who talks about gates and blast radius, not the one who talks about how smart the model is.
  • Reviewing other people's agents. Organizations are staffing exactly this: someone who can audit an agent before it ships and say what to remove or gate. The Practical is that skill, on a page.
  • Not being the cautionary tale. The headlines about agents deleting databases and leaking data are all preventable with the four habits here. Knowing them is what keeps your name out of the incident report.
