13 JULY 2026 · v1.0

Building with AI, first edition

  • 14 July 2026. Course listed in the catalog and activated: the Agent Foundry and the Workshop kit shipped, Aira's voice restored across every page, a modern high-tech visual refresh, and an agent-loop certificate. The lecture film is in production.
  • Twelve modules across the four habits that separate the agents that ship from the ones that make the news: scope well (the right job, and the do-not-automate list), wire well (tools, connectors, and MCP), test well (golden sets and regression testing), and supervise well (least privilege, blast radius, prompt injection, and the human gate).
  • Every number and tool re-verified live on 13 July 2026 against the primary source. Several widely repeated figures turned out to be stale or misattributed; this course carries the corrected versions with their dates, and sources the "most pilots fail" claim to the MIT NANDA (2025) and IDC (2026) studies rather than the misquoted numbers circulating online.
  • AI Lab of five games, including Break It, a hands-on prompt-injection hunt, and Blast Radius, where the only winning path is to give least privilege and keep a human at the gate.
  • The Dry Run: a deterministic in-browser agent simulator where you configure an agent's tools and gates, run it against a poisoned email, and watch your guardrail choices decide whether the injection causes a breach. No cloud, no account, nothing sent anywhere.
  • The Practical (honors exam): audit one real agent run, find the injection it obeyed and the step it skipped, and write the fix, which automatically fails if it leaves the agent able to act with no human gate, or relies only on prompting the model to behave.
  • The thesis, defended in every module: making an agent look impressive takes an afternoon; making one you can trust on real inputs is the actual job, and an agent can do the work but a human always owns the outcome.
Why a changelog on a free course? Because this course tells you that an automation you never tested is a claim you cannot trust, and it would be absurd to be one. Every number and tool in this course is a dated fact with a source on the resources page. When a tool is renamed, a benchmark moves, or an integration count changes, the fix lands here with the date attached. If you spot something stale before we do, say so on the community board: that is not a complaint, it is the course working.
